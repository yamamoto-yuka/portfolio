BioGeek

Interface Planning and Design
Case Study
(school project)

0, Background

BioGeek is an edtech startup that makes educational video content for learning biology. This is a story I created to provide UI design suggestions on how users can reduce barriers to e-learning and purchasing online content.

Features of this company

  • They stand out because of the quality of their instructors.
  • They target a mature demographic.
  • They want to convey a sense of wonder and professionalism at the same time.

Requirement Design parts

  • A home page
  • A shop page
  • A product page

What I Did

  • Competitive research
  • User research
  • Interactive design

1, THE DISCOVERY

The assignment was to create a website that incorporated a UI perspective, but I also learned about UX perspectives in order to look at the user experience from a broader angle, such as what are the problems in this industry and what problems users are having.

Initially, among the educational video content, I wanted to check the large market information, so I decided to refer to research and survey information about the MOOC.

( MOOC stands for Massive Open Online Course and represents an instructional approach that permits hundreds of thousands of students to access online courses anywhere around the world and typically free of charge. )

Understanding the problem on this market

I used as resources some articles that consider the problems facing the MOOC market and their solutions.

The majority of learners will either drop out of the course entirely or complete the course without any transferable credits.

Think creatively and innovatively and to explore new pedagogical practices for learners motivation, participatory learning and also to satisfy individual learners need.

Refer to Dr. P. Malliga’s “A SURVEY ON MOOC PROVIDERS FOR HIGHER EDUCATION”

One of the major disadvantages of online learning was maintaining motivation.

I also looked up articles with detailed surveys to find out what causes users to lose motivation.

While those who completed their MOOC successfully did not mention the role of human (instructor or peer) interaction, this was a major theme that emerged from the comments of those that did not complete. Participants mentioned feeling isolated and alone with poor communication with instructor and peers; they described being unmotivated to continue due to low interaction and poor feedback and communication with both instructors and peers, with no teamwork or group interaction; they complained that the instructor did not praise or motivate them after quizzes; and they stated that instructors did not engage learners in discussion or facilitate brainstorming.

For example:

“I did not continue with the Coursera course as I was demotivated due to poor instructor's feedback”

“The instructor did not praise learners”

“The instructor didn't … engage us in discussions”

Another clear theme from those who completed concerned the packaging of material, especially video, into small chunks which could be flexibly accessed. For example participants praised the use of short videos and course organisation that allowed them to skip some sessions. Conversely, participants who did not complete emphasised the presence of very long videos (of an hour or more) which were both boring and difficult to load due to connectivity issues especially from mobile devices, and which made it difficult to scan for relevant content.

For example:

“Contents are overwhelming and not given in small chunks”

“Course was not easy to scan in terms of content”

Refer to Kate S. Hone, Ghada R. El's “Exploring the factors affecting MOOC retention: A survey study”

Based on the results of the user survey, I examined what users wanted.

  • Accurate feedback from the instructor
  • The importance of motivational communication from the instructor to maintain motivation
  • Time for discussion and brain-streaming with the instructor
  • Communication with peers
  • The importance of packaging educational materials, especially videos, into small chunks that can be accessed flexibly

Interviews

Next step, I conducted interviews with students and working professionals who work for engineering firms.

Interview target:
As for students, I targeted graduate students in engineering (mechanical engineering, science and engineering (chemistry and life sciences departments), and environmental engineering). 5 students
As for working professionals, I targeted those working in science-related technical fields (chemical research and development, plant design, etc.). 5 persons

Following were some of the questions asked to them on a phone call/google meet interview:

  • Purpose of using video learning
  • What is the advantage of video content?
  • If you have experienced interruptions in the middle of content, what were the reasons for the interruptions?
  • What devices do you use to search for and view video content?
  • When you are studying, what do you wish there was such a function or site?

Then I grouped the most common responses from interviews and observed different aspects of the problem.

Category and filter functions

Many people found it painful to find the right product for them. I also found that many people check the contents of multiple reviews.

Content Details

Many people said they would like the ability to view a sample video or view a few of the real products before purchasing.

Mobile app, offline support

The majority of people used mobile or tablet devices to view the videos during the gap time. The users were concerned about an offline function and mobile application availability.

Through these researchs, I was able to understand what kind of content and services users are looking for.

Competitors

The next step was to understand the competitor's site and examine more specifically what the problems were from a UI perspective.

I did my research in detail on Udemy, where I can see a lot of customer reviews.

There were many critical reviews, including the following

  • Difficulty in viewing categories
  • System is slow, no offline operation
  • Unable to obtain an approved certificate

2, THE EXPLORE

Persona & User Journey

Based on interviews and research of Udemy, I created my persona.

I also created a user journey map based on that persona and summarized the pain points.

MIA is persona as a student user.

ISSAC is persona as a working user.

Finding Further Pain Points

In order to make sure that the personas and user journeys I came up with were appropriate, I asked friends and actually used sites such as Udemy myself to see if I really felt the pain points I considered.

Based on these verifications, I have summarized the key points that should be applied in the information architecture and wireframes.

  • Category
    • Create detailed, easy-to-read categories that are useful to mature users.
  • Filter function
    • Create a filter function that allows users to find content that suits them.
  • Hierarchy
    • Reduce the number of pages as much as possible so that users can reach the desired page.
  • Breadcrumbs
    • Display a breadcrumb list on every page so that users can quickly see where they are.
  • Details of Contents
    • Provide appropriate information that users want to confirm before making a purchase.
